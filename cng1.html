Countable and Uncountable Nouns

In English, some nouns are not countable, because they are considered a whole, mass or a category.

Countable Nouns

What we can count: be singular or plural

Examples: a car, three cars
a sister, two sisters

Uncountable Nouns

What cannot be separated or counted:

  • Some ideas: experience, advice, news, progress
  • Materials and substances: water, rice, gold, milk
  • Weather words: thunder, rain
  • Groups or collections: furniture, equipment

Uncountable Nouns Used Countably

Measures and Examples

Sometimes uncountable nouns are used countably, to mean 'a measure of something' or 'a type or example of something':

Can I have three teas and one coffee, please? (three cups of tea and one cup of coffee)

A: How many sugars do you want in your tea? (How many spoons of sugar?)

B: Just one, please.

Quantifying Uncountable Nouns

We can quantify an uncountable noun by using quantifier words. Here are some examples:

Milk: a carton of milk, a gallon of milk, a liter of milk
Bread: a loaf of bread, a slice of bread, a piece of bread
Butter: a stick of butter, a tub of butter, a pat of butter
Cheese: a slice of cheese, a block of cheese, a piece of cheese
Juice: a bottle of juice, a glass of juice, a carton of juice
Rice: a bag of rice, a cup of rice, a bowl of rice
Flour: a bag of flour, a cup of flour, a pound of flour
Sugar: a bag of sugar, a teaspoon of sugar, a cup of sugar
Coffee: a cup of coffee, a pot of coffee, a bag of coffee
Tea: a cup of tea, a pot of tea, a box of tea
Pasta: a box of pasta, a plate of pasta, a serving of pasta
Yogurt: a tub of yogurt, a cup of yogurt, a spoonful of yogurt
Meat: a pound of meat, a slice of meat, a piece of meat
